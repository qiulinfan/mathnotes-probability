Problem 1

Suppose that the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a random variable \(F: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is strictly increasing and continuous. Let \(U\) be a random variable with the uniform distribution on \((0,1)\) and define \[X:=F^{-1}(U)\] Show that \(X\) has CDF equal to \(F\). This exercise shows us how to construct a random variable with given distribution, assuming that we have a uniform random variable.

Since \(F\) is strictly increasing and continuous, it has an inverse function \(F^{-1}\) on its range, and \(F^{-1}\) is also strictly increasing. Thus for any \(x,y\in \mathbb{R}\), \[F^{-1}(y) \leq x \iff y \leq (F^{-1})^{-1}(x) = F(x)\] Therefore for any \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), we have \[\{x \mid X(x) \le x\}=\{ x \mid F^{-1}(U(x))\le x\} = \{x \mid U(x) \le F(x)\}\] Therefore \[\mathbb{P}(X \le x)=\mathbb{P}(U(x) \le F(x))\] Since \(U \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0,1)\) and for a CDF we have \(F(x)\in[0,1]\), we get \[\mathbb{P}(U(x) \le F(x)) = F(x)\] Thus for all \(x\) \(\mathbb{P}(X \le x)=F(x)\), i.e., the CDF of \(X\) equals \(F\).

Problem 2

A gas station fills its tank completely once a week. Let the weekly sales volume (in thousands of liters) be a random variable with density \[f(x)= \begin{cases}a(1-x)^4, & x \in(0,1), \\ 0, & \text { otherwise } \end{cases}\] Find the constant \(a\). What should be the tank capacity so that the probability of running out of fuel during a given week is \(1 / 100\) ?

Since the density integrates to 1, \[1=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx=\int_0^1 a(1-x)^4\,dx = a \int_0^1 (1-x)^4\,dx\] Let \(u=1-x\), then \[\int_0^1 (1-x)^4 dx=\int_0^1 u^4 du=\frac{1}{5}\]So \(\frac{1}{5}a=1\), which gives \[a=5\] Now we look for the tank capacity \(c\) such that \(\mathbb{P}(X>c)=\frac{1}{100}\).
Let the tank capacity be \(c\) (in thousands of liters). Running out of fuel in a week occurs when sales exceed \(c\), i.e., the event \(\{X>c\}\). We need \[\mathbb{P}(X>c)=\frac{1}{100}\] Since \(a=5\), \[\mathbb{P}(X>c)=\int_c^1 5(1-x)^4\,dx\] Again let \(u=1-x\), then we have \[\int_c^1 5(1-x)^4 dx = 5\int_{1-c}^{0} u^4(-du)=5\int_0^{1-c} u^4 du =5\cdot \frac{(1-c)^5}{5}=(1-c)^5\] Therefore \[(1-c)^5=\frac{1}{100} \implies 1-c=100^{-1/5}=10^{-2/5} \implies c=1-10^{-2/5}\] So the tank capacity should be \(1-10^{-2/5}\) thousand liters.

Problem 3

Let the random variable \(X\) have density \[f_X(x)= \begin{cases}\frac{1}{2 x^2}, & |x| \geq 1, \\ 0, & |x|<1 .\end{cases}\] Find the probability density function of \(Y:=X^2\) and compute the probability \(\mathbb{P}(2 Y+3 \leq 10)\).

Since \(f_X(x)=0\) for \(|x|<1\), we have \(\mathbb P(|X|\ge 1)=1\). Hence \(Y=X^2\ge 1\) almost surely, so \(F_Y(y)=0\) for \(y<1\) and therefore \(f_Y(y)=0\) for \(y<1\) (a.e.).

For \(y\ge 1\), \[F_Y(y)=\mathbb P(X^2\le y)=\mathbb P(-\sqrt y\le X\le \sqrt y) =\int_{-\sqrt y}^{-1}\frac{1}{2x^2}\,dx+\int_{1}^{\sqrt y}\frac{1}{2x^2}\,dx\] Compute each integral: \[\int_{1}^{\sqrt y}\frac{1}{2x^2}\,dx=\frac12\int_{1}^{\sqrt y}x^{-2}\,dx =\frac12\Big[-x^{-1}\Big]_{1}^{\sqrt y} =\frac12\left(1-\frac{1}{\sqrt y}\right)\] and similarly \(\int_{-\sqrt y}^{-1}\frac{1}{2x^2}\,dx = \frac12\left(1-\frac{1}{\sqrt y}\right)\) since the function is even. Therefore, for \(y\ge 1\), \[F_Y(y)=1-\frac{1}{\sqrt y}\] Combining both cases we have \[F_Y(y)= \begin{cases} 0, & y<1,\\ 1-\dfrac{1}{\sqrt y} & y\ge 1 \end{cases}\] Notice that on \(y \geq 1\), \(F_Y(y)\) is differentiable (except on \(y=1\)): \[F_Y'(y)=\frac{d}{dy}\left(1-y^{-1/2}\right)=\frac12\,y^{-3/2}\] So consider the function \[g(y)= \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{2y^{3/2}}, & y\ge 1,\\[6pt] 0, & y<1. \end{cases}\] Then for \(x<1\), \[\int_{-\infty}^x g(y)\,dy=0=F_Y(x)\] and for \(x\ge 1\) \[\int_{-\infty}^x g(y)\,dy=\int_{1}^x \frac{1}{2y^{3/2}}\,dy =\left[-y^{-1/2}\right]_{1}^{x}=1-\frac{1}{\sqrt x}=F_Y(x)\] This shows that \(Y\) is absolutely continuous and \(g\) is a probability density of \(Y\). Hence \[f_Y(y)= \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{2y^{3/2}}, & y\ge 1,\\ 0, & y<1 \end{cases}\] Now we compute \(\mathbb P(2Y+3\le 10)\).

We have \(2Y+3\le 10 \iff Y\le \frac72\). Thus \[\mathbb P(2Y+3\le 10)=\mathbb P\left(Y\le \frac72\right)=F_Y\!\left(\frac72\right) =1-\frac{1}{\sqrt{7/2}} =1-\sqrt{\frac{2}{7}}\] Thus, \[\mathbb P(2Y+3\le 10)=1-\sqrt{\frac{2}{7}}\]

Problem 4

Let the random variable \(X\) have density \(f\), which is symmetric about \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}\), that is, \(f(\mu+x)= f(\mu-x)\), for all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\). Show that \(\mathbb{P}(X \leq \mu)=\mathbb{P}(X \geq \mu)\). If in addition \(\mathbb{E}|X|<\infty\), show that \(\mathbb{E}(X)=\mu\). Can you use this observation if \(X \sim N(0,1)\) ?

Since \(X\) has density \(f\), \[\mathbb{P}(X\le \mu)=\int_{-\infty}^{\mu} f(t)\,dt\] Let \(t=\mu-x\) so that \(dt=-dx\). Then \[\int_{-\infty}^{\mu} f(t)\,dt =\int_{\infty}^{0} f(\mu-x)(-dx) =\int_{0}^{\infty} f(\mu-x)\,dx\] Similarly, \[\mathbb{P}(X\ge \mu)=\int_{\mu}^{\infty} f(t)\,dt =\int_{0}^{\infty} f(\mu+x)\,dx\] By symmetry \(f(\mu-x)=f(\mu+x)\) for all \(x\), hence the two integrals are equal, i.e. proved \[\mathbb{P}(X\le \mu)=\mathbb{P}(X\ge \mu)\]

If \(\mathbb{E}|X|<\infty\), then \(\mathbb{E}[X]=\mu\) for some \(\mu\in\mathbb{R}\), We want to show that this \(\mu\) is the same as the one in the symmetry condition. Consider \(\mathbb{E}[X-\mu]\). Since \(\mathbb{E}|X|<\infty\), we also have \(\mathbb{E}|X-\mu|<\infty\), so the following integral is well-defined: \[\mathbb{E}[X-\mu]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (t-\mu) f(t)\,dt\] Let \(t=\mu+x\); then \[\mathbb{E}[X-\mu]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\, f(\mu+x)\,dx\] Define \(g(x):=f(\mu+x)\). The symmetry condition \(f(\mu+x)=f(\mu-x)\) implies that \(g\) is an even function, thus \(xg(x)\) is an odd function. Since \(\int |x|g(x)\,dx<\infty\), we may integrate over symmetric limits to get \[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} xg(x)\,dx=0\] Therefore \(\mathbb{E}[X-\mu]=0\), thus \[\mathbb{E}[X]=\mu\]

Application to \(X\sim N(0,1)\): Since the standard normal density \(\varphi(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}\) satisfies \(\varphi(0+x)=\varphi(0-x)\), so it is symmetric about \(\mu=0\). Hence \[\mathbb{P}(X\le 0)=\mathbb{P}(X\ge 0)=\tfrac12 \quad\text{and}\quad \mathbb{E}[X]=0\]

Problem 5

An airline has observed that \(5 \%\) of ticket holders do not show up for their flight. Todayâ€™s flight has an airplane with 200 seats, and the airline has sold 203 tickets. What is the probability that the airline will not be able to accommodate a ticketed passenger? Assume that, for each passenger \(i\), the event \(A_i\) that passenger \(i\) shows up is independent of all others, for \(1 \leq i \leq 203\).

Let \(S\) be the number of passengers who show up. The condition indicates that \(S\) is a binomial random variable with parameters \(n=203\) and \(p=0.95\): \[S\sim\mathrm{Binomial}(n=203,p=0.95)\] The airline cannot accommodate everyone exactly when more than 200 passengers show up, i.e. \[\mathbb{P}(\text{cannot accommodate})= \mathbb{P}(S\ge 201)=\sum_{k=201}^{203} \binom{203}{k}(0.95)^k(0.05)^{203-k}\] Equivalently, letting \(N:=203-S\) be the number of no-shows, we have \(N\sim\mathrm{Binomial}(203,0.05)\) and \[\mathbb{P}(S\ge 201)=\mathbb{P}(N\le 2)=\sum_{j=0}^{2}\binom{203}{j}(0.05)^j(0.95)^{203-j}\] Numerically we can calculate \[\mathbb{P}(\text{cannot accommodate})\approx 0.206\%\]

Problem 6

Consider a sequence of tosses of a fair die. We continue tossing until both outcomes 3 and 4 have appeared at least once. For example, one possible sequence of results is \[5,1,1,4,6,5,4,2,6,3,\] and we then stop. Let \(X\) be the number of tosses required (in this example, \(X=10\) ). What is the expected value of the random variable \(X\) ?

We can decompose the waiting time into two stages.

Stage 1: wait until the first time we see either 3 or 4: On each toss, the probability to get a 3 or 4 is \(2/6=1/3\). Hence the number of tosses \(T_1\) until the first occurrence of \(\{3,4\}\) is geometric with success probability \(1/3\), so \[\mathbb{E}[T_1]=\frac{1}{1/3}=3\]

Stage 2: after seeing one of them, wait until we see the other: Once 3 has appeared, each subsequent toss produces a 4 with probability \(1/6\); otherwise we are still missing a 4. Thus the additional waiting time \(T_2\) is geometric with success probability \(1/6\), so \[\mathbb{E}[T_2]=\frac{1}{1/6}=6\]

Since \(X=T_1+T_2\), by linearity of expectation we get \[\mathbb{E}[X]=\mathbb{E}[T_1]+\mathbb{E}[T_2]=3+6=9\]
